SLOPE: South Lunar Observational Prospecting Expedition

Skills: Aerospace systems, space architechture, FEA analysis, system integration, proposal writing, prototyping and iteration, algorithm development

Role: Mission Lead and Principal Mechanical Engineer

As co-lead for the Columbia Space Initiative's RASC-AL mission, I led a team of 15 students in ideation, engineering design, simulation, and early manufacturing efforts for a dual-rover lunar architechture to explore permanently shadowed regions (PSRs) for valuable volatiles.

The mission architecture consists of four main systems: the Crater Rim Energy System Transporter (CREST), the Tethered Radial vOlatiles Garage Hub (TROGH), the MObile Volatile ExporteR (MOVER), and two prospecting Sampling Ice Rovers (SIRs) named SIR-Jack and SIR-Jill. During launch, the CREST, MOVER, and both SIRs will be stored safely within the large TROGH, awaiting deployment via connecting tethers. After landing, the TROGH will position the CREST on the Shackleton rim utilizing a forklift mechanism and stabilizers for secure detachment. CREST will then drill itself securely into the lunar regolith using its four aluminum Kevlar anchors, perform communications testing, and begin solar power production. Once the power base has been secured and deemed fully functional, the TROGH system will begin to unravel the tether connecting to the CREST and begin its fully autonomous descent into the crater.

As the TROGH descends, it will utilize its housed drilling mechanism to periodically drive stakes into the ground, which are continuously connected via a tether. The tether is pulled by a winch on TROGH, allowing it to rappel the rocky descent and increase stabilization. Once the TROGH has completed its descent, the two SIRs will exit the TROGH and begin prospecting, using its connecting tether to stabilize its trajectory. Both the TROGH and the SIRs are equipped with HazCams, NavCams, laser range scanner sensors, and photoelectric sensors to full visibility in dark lunar craters. Inspired by NASA's SHERPA AI software, our system will build on collected data from NASA's VIPER propsecting mission to create optimal paths. Autonomous direction finding and motor control were implemented on a Raspberry Pi microprocessor in Python.

Solar power will be implemented at the CREST, actively harnessing the continual sunlight of regions of Shacketon’s rim. Solar panels will be mounted on three sides of the CREST, as one side will be reserved for the tether and sample deposits. Power from solar panels on the CREST will be transmitted down into the crater through a high-efficiency laser, and the TROGH and SIRs will be equipped with laser PV converters to convert the power from the lasers back into electricity to power their operations and batteries. These converters are made up of many small x-band antennas, each connected to a rectifier diode, that will convert the laser to DC power. This will allow the SIRs to recharge without returning to the TROGH, extending their operating time. The CREST’s laser beaming system can transmit more than 10 kW, allowing it to both charge batteries and actively power the operations of individual SIRs and the TROGH.
